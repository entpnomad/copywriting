# Copywriting

Copywriting is not trickery. It's the art of simple communication: say one true thing, make it land in two seconds, make it so it sticks. The hucksters gaming your psychology with fake scarcity and bonus stacks are doing something else - and worse. This skill is the craft of clear, concrete, differentiated communication, distilled from Harry Dry (Marketing Examples).

**Copywriting is arguing.** The conclusion is "buy this / believe this." A good ad is the best argument for it, made in the fewest words.

## The 3-line test (run this on every line)

For any headline, tagline, or sentence that has to carry weight, ask three questions:

1. **Can I visualize it?** Close your eyes - can you see it?
2. **Can I falsify it?** Is it provably true or false?
3. **Can nobody else say it?** Could a competitor sign the exact same line?

**Three nos = you've written rubbish. Three yeses = you're onto something.**

The benchmark: *"New Balance, worn by supermodels in London and dads in Ohio."* You can see it. It's true. No other shoe can say it (Prada isn't on dads in Ohio; supermodels aren't in Reebok). Contrast the bin: *"Don't just get a job. Change an entire industry."* Can't see it, can't falsify it, any recruiter could sign it.

## 1. Make it concrete (so it can be visualized)

If you can't see it, you won't remember it. Concrete things you can drop on your foot (a charging pitbull, a leg of lamb). Abstract things fade away (a "better way," a "seamless transition"). People remember the ones they can picture.

**The zoom-in drill.** Draw a line down a page. Write `abstract` at the top, `concrete` at the bottom. Put the vague word up top, then rewrite it, asking "what do I actually mean?" until you hit a concrete object.

> "Regain fitness" → what do you mean, regain? → off the couch → what fitness? → running → how far? → 5K → **"Couch to 5K"**

That's the most downloaded fitness program ever - and it's just the abstract idea made concrete.

## 2. Make it falsifiable (point, don't talk)

A sentence that's provably true or false makes the reader sit up - you've put your head on the chopping block. *"The earth spins around the sun"* gets you house arrest. *"The earth has a harmonious connection with a celestial object"* gets you ignored.

**Get off the adjective trail.** Don't describe - point at something true.

| Adjective (talking) | Fact (pointing) |
|---|---|
| He's good-looking | Looks like Ryan Gosling, 6'2" |
| He's intelligent | Reads on the tube |
| Gold is a great investment | [the 50-year gold chart] |
| It's unequal | [the graph showing the gap] |

**If in doubt, give me a fact.** Most people open their mouths and say wallpaper - word-shaped air. A fact guarantees you've said *something* true, and behind every fact there's a story you can tell. ("At 60mph the loudest noise in this Rolls-Royce is the electric clock" - pulled from a motor magazine, increased sales 50%.)

Pointing takes research. That's exactly why it's better - a competitor would have to do the same work to copy it.

## 3. Make it ownable (a competitor can't sign it)

*"Never write an ad a competitor can sign."* If a rival could paste their logo on your line, the line says nothing about you. Forcing ownability makes you look harder at what you're actually selling.

> Volvo: *"Your car has five numbers on the speedometer. Volvo has six. One could get the impression that the people who made your car lack a little confidence."* The only way to verify it is to get in the Volvo. Visual aligns with text. Nobody else can say it.

## The 2-second test (One Mississippi, Two Mississippi)

Show the copy. Count: "One Mississippi, two Mississippi." If they don't get it inside two seconds, it failed. Sometimes you know more in 2 seconds than in 2 hours. (Caveat: a line your *in-crowd* gets instantly and the out-crowd has to ask about is often a feature - "you're going to need a smaller cabinet" for Athletic Greens. If everyone gets it, you may be playing too safe.)

## The process: who → what → how

When you sit down, you're moving the reader from their current attitude (point A) to the desired attitude (point B). Two telephone poles; your job is to string the wire. Three pieces:

**1. Who are you talking to?** A tourist glancing at a café board needs *"Coffee + pastry = £5"*, not 17 croissant options and an Instagram handle. Snapchat spent $7M on a Super Bowl ad ("more Snapchat, less social media") aimed at 39-year-olds who'd never use Snapchat. Get the reader wrong and nothing else matters.

**2. Have something to say.** This is upstream of wording. Build from a belief, a vision, or something you think is wrong with the world - then the copy has a spine. *"Couch to 5K"* came from a Boston TV producer, not Nike, and stuck because it was good. The Secret Footballer outsold Beckham, Lampard, Gerrard and Owen *combined* by asking "what can I do that no one else is doing that people care about" - and then positioning a mediocre product brilliantly.

**3. Say it well.** Now make it memorable: alliteration, juxtaposition, metonymy. *"Hinge, the dating app designed to be deleted"* - the brief was "an app for people who want relationships, tired of dating apps." The writer turned that into the line. *"1,000 songs in your pocket"* - it's a media player, but "pocket" is visual, surprising, punchy (that's metonymy: a vivid stand-in for the literal term).

## Conflict is the engine

We judge everything relatively. Pickle juice makes orange juice taste sweeter. So set up a contrast - draw a line down the page and write two opposites. Three kinds of enemy:

- **Approaches** - a different way of solving the same problem (the corporate way vs. the copywriting way)
- **Beliefs** - I believe this, you believe that
- **Competitors** - Mac vs. PC, Corvette vs. Volvo

Hinge against every other dating app. Loom against every other screen recorder. Before/after, problem/solution, here's-how-they-do-it/here's-how-we-do-it. Find the "but" in the story without writing the word "but."

## Positioning

Two questions that crack most positioning:

- **What do you replace?** Athletic Greens → "you're going to need a smaller cabinet" (it replaces the whole vitamin shelf).
- **What can you do that nobody else can, that people care about?** Loom started in the "screen recorder" bucket. Then: "remote communication sucks" → **async video messaging**. They created the category and are now worth more than the screen-recorder bucket combined.

## Rewrite until it's simple

You don't write simply. You *rewrite* simply.

- **Kaplan's law of words:** any words that aren't working for you are working against you. Cut harder than feels comfortable.
- **The paragraph test:** pull one sentence out. A good paragraph should break. If it still works, that sentence was never needed.
- **Write versions.** Copy-paste-rewrite the same line 4-5 ways. The freedom to do it badly is what lets you find the different, right one. Bonus: people give far better feedback choosing between three versions than judging one.
- **Strength of an idea is inversely proportional to its scope.** "And" on a landing page is usually a warning. *"We make jeans. That's it."* beats "jeans and tees and socks and we have dressing rooms."

## Copy and design are one thing

Never write in a Word doc and paste it in later. Write in the medium: the newsletter in the newsletter tool, the ad in Figma, the billboard mocked up among other billboards. A line that's one line in your draft might spill to three in reality. See it exactly as the reader will - including all the other ads fighting for the same attention.

## Structure = dividing lines + parallelism

Break the big scary thing into 1, 2, 3. "Copywriting" → who you're talking to / what you're saying / how you say it. "Get 10,000 readers" → first 100 (things that don't scale) / next 9,900 (momentum). A reframe that splits one thing into two, or merges two into one, is itself interesting. Chapters are what make a book holdable; structure is what makes copy holdable.

## Newsletter craft (if writing one)

A newsletter is less about the *news*, more about the *letter*. Three moves that work:

1. **Open with time, place, what's going on.** Look out the window. *"Live from London. Weather update: trees shuddering, majestic hail."* It writes itself, and Twitter can't fake it.
2. **Five or six things, each built on conflict** - before/after, problem/solution, them/us.
3. **No paragraph longer than two lines.** Short paragraphs are easy to swing between; three lines usually means you haven't explained yourself yet; lines need room to breathe.

## What this is NOT

- **Not persuasion-by-trick.** No fake urgency, no bonus stacks, no psychological gaming. If the argument isn't real, don't make it.
- **Not big claims.** "Learn copywriting and become a millionaire" is worse than "increase conversions from 1% to 2%." Believable and specific beats grand and hollow.
- **Juxtaposition is not negation.** "Show what it isn't" (Mac vs. PC, "designed to be deleted") is the *enemy/contrast* move and it's good. That is different from defining yourself through a string of negations ("This isn't X. It's not Y. It's not Z.") - which is empty. Use the contrast to make a positive point land, not as your whole identity.

## Handoff to tone-of-voice

This skill builds the *line* - concrete, falsifiable, ownable. It does not decide *whose voice* speaks it. Once the copy works, run it through **[[tone-of-voice]]** for vocabulary, banned phrases, and personality. Two jobs: copywriting = the argument and the craft; tone-of-voice = the identity.

## Worked example: 25 rewrites of one ad

Harry's process for a course landing-page ad, start to finish - this is what "rewrite until it's simple" actually looks like:

1. **Seed (steal a good idea):** "The difference between 1% and 2% is not 1%, it's 100%." A great argument for copywriting - doubling a 1% landing page to 2% doubles growth.
2. **Steal a structure:** the Volkswagen "ordinary car vs. Volkswagen" checklist layout. Two columns, an enemy, simplicity shown not stated. (What you take in as a reader is what you have to draw on. Collect excellence.)
3. **Set up the conflict:** "Want to grow twice as fast? You have two choices..." Corporate way vs. copywriting way.
4. **Fix what irritates you:** the right-hand line ran two lines. Cut it to one "by hook or by crook" - "learn to write" got promoted to the header.
5. **Solve the new problem you just made:** headers lost parallelism. Spitball replacements - "go fool Zuckerberg," "roll Monopoly dice," nonsense - to get the dirty water out of the tap until "Throw Money and Pray" comes out clean.
6. **Get feedback on versions**, not one draft. Settle on the winner.
7. **Add design last:** bold the rhythm ("twice... twice... both"), commission the illustration, iterate it (the praying guy should look like an idiot; the writer gets a typewriter and a lightbulb).

Final: *"Want to grow twice as fast? You have two choices. Throw money and pray [raise twice the cash, hire twice the staff, spend twice as much on ads, cross both your fingers] — or learn copywriting [increase conversions from 1% to 2%. That's twice as many]."* Two days, 25 rewrites. You can't write that cold. You build it piece by piece.

## On AI and taste

AI is a tool - only as good as your taste. What it can't do: **see, believe, or have lived something.** Ogilvy could write the Rolls-Royce clock line because someone sat in the car. Bukowski wrote *Post Office* after 20 years as a postman. A writer arranges words in an order not laid out before; a predictor reassembles orders that already exist. So go out, notice things, build conviction, hold a high standard. *Your work is nothing but your standards.* 9/10 or it doesn't ship.
