# Copywriting

Copywriting is making an argument in the fewest words. The conclusion is "buy this" or "believe this"; the line is the shortest path to it. Not trickery - if the argument isn't true, don't make it.

## The test: run it on every line that carries weight

For any headline, tagline, or key sentence, ask three questions:

1. **Can I visualize it?** Close your eyes - can you see it?
2. **Can I falsify it?** Is it provably true or false?
3. **Can nobody else say it?** Could a competitor sign the exact same line?

Three nos, rewrite it. Three yeses, keep it.

Passes: *"New Balance, worn by supermodels in London and dads in Ohio"* - you can see it, it's true, no rival can claim it. Fails: *"Don't just get a job. Change an entire industry"* - can't see it, can't verify it, any recruiter could sign it.

## 1. Make it concrete

Abstract fades; concrete sticks. If they can't picture it, they won't remember it.

Drill: write the vague word, then rewrite it - "what do I actually mean?" - until you hit something you could drop on your foot.
> "Regain fitness" → off the couch → running → 5K → **"Couch to 5K"**

## 2. Make it falsifiable - point, don't talk

Get off the adjective trail. Don't describe - point at something provably true.

| Adjective (talking) | Fact (pointing) |
|---|---|
| good-looking | looks like Ryan Gosling, 6'2" |
| intelligent | reads on the tube |
| great investment | the 50-year price chart |
| unequal | the graph of the gap |

**If in doubt, give a fact.** It's precise, it's checkable, and it forces research a competitor would have to repeat. Behind every fact is a story you can tell.

## 3. Make it ownable

If a rival could paste their logo on your line, it says nothing about you. Forcing ownability makes you look harder at what you actually sell.
> Volvo: *"Your car has five numbers on the speedometer. Volvo has six."* The only way to check is to get in the car.

## The 2-second test

Show the copy. If they don't get it in two seconds, it failed. Exception: a line your *target* gets instantly while outsiders have to ask is doing its job (Athletic Greens: "you're going to need a smaller cabinet"). If everyone gets it immediately, you may be playing too safe.

## The process: who → what → how

Move the reader from their current attitude (A) to the desired one (B):

1. **Who are you talking to?** Match the message to the reader's moment. A tourist scanning a café board needs "Coffee + pastry = £5", not 17 options. Wrong reader, nothing else matters.
2. **Have something to say.** Build from a belief or point of view before polishing words. A spine beats clever phrasing with none.
3. **Say it well.** Make it memorable - alliteration, juxtaposition, a vivid stand-in for the literal word. *"Hinge, the dating app designed to be deleted."* *"1,000 songs in your pocket"* (not "media player").

## Conflict is the engine

We judge everything relatively, so set up a contrast. Three kinds of enemy:
- **Approach** - a different way to solve the same problem (the corporate way vs. the copywriting way)
- **Belief** - I hold this, you hold that
- **Competitor** - Mac vs. PC

Before/after, problem/solution. Build the "but" into the story without writing the word.

## Positioning

Two questions crack most of it:
- **What do you replace?** Athletic Greens replaces the vitamin shelf → "a smaller cabinet."
- **What can you do that nobody else can, that people care about?** Loom left the "screen recorder" bucket for "async video messaging" - created the category, now worth more than the bucket.

## Rewrite until it's simple

You don't write simply; you rewrite simply.
- **Cut hard.** Any word not working for you is working against you.
- **Paragraph test.** Pull one sentence out. If the paragraph still works, that sentence was dead weight.
- **Write versions.** Rewrite the same line 4-5 ways; permission to do it badly surfaces the good one. People give better feedback choosing among three than judging one.
- **Narrow beats broad.** "And" on a landing page is a warning sign. *"We make jeans. That's it."*

## Copy and design are one

Write in the final medium, not a Word doc: the newsletter in the newsletter tool, the ad in the layout, the billboard mocked up among other billboards. See it exactly as the reader will, competing for the same attention.

## Structure

Break the big thing into 1, 2, 3 with dividing lines and parallelism. "Copywriting" → who you talk to / what you say / how you say it. "Get 10,000 readers" → first 100 / next 9,900. A reframe that splits one thing into two, or merges two into one, is itself interesting.

## Newsletter craft

1. **Open with time, place, what's happening.** Concrete and unfakeable.
2. **Several items, each built on a conflict** - before/after, them/us.
3. **No paragraph over two lines.** Longer usually means you haven't explained it yet.

## What this is NOT

- **Not persuasion by trick.** No fake urgency, no bonus stacks. If the argument isn't real, drop it.
- **Not big claims.** "Increase conversions from 1% to 2%" beats "become a millionaire." Believable and specific wins.
- **Juxtaposition is not negation.** "Show what it isn't" (Mac vs. PC) is the contrast move and it works. Defining yourself through a string of negations ("not X, not Y, not Z") is empty. Use contrast to land a positive point, not as your identity.

## The rewrite loop, in practice

How a line actually gets built:
1. **Start from a seed worth keeping** - "the difference between 1% and 2% isn't 1%, it's 100%."
2. **Borrow a structure that fits** - a two-column "their way vs. our way" layout.
3. **Set up the conflict** - "Want to grow twice as fast? Two choices..."
4. **Fix what irritates you** - a line that wraps to two gets cut to one, by any means.
5. **Spitball replacements** until a clean one lands.
6. **Get feedback on versions, not one draft.**
7. **Add design last** - bold the rhythm, commission and iterate the image.

Cold, you can't write the final line. You build it.

## On AI

A tool, only as good as your taste. It can't see, believe, or have lived anything, so it recombines what already exists. Your edge: notice things firsthand, hold a point of view, keep a high bar. Your work is nothing but your standards.
